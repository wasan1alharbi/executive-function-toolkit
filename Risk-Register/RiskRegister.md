# Risk Register

A Risk Register is actually one of the easiest ways to pick up "Project Management" marks in your dissertation. While it can feel complicated to predict all the associated risks, this can be easily managed. Think of it as a way to say: *I know things will probably go wrong, and here is exactly what I will do when they do.*

---

## Where does this go?

Usually, this belongs in one of two places:
1. **The Methodology / Project Planning Chapter:** If your table is small (3-5 risks), drop it right into the main text to show how you are managing the project.
2. **The Appendix:** If your university requires a massive, detailed table, put it at the very end of your document. In your main text, just write one sentence: *"A full risk assessment was conducted to ensure project viability and can be found in Appendix A."*

## The two things everyone mixes up

When writing your table, you will need a "Mitigation" and a "Contingency". Here is the difference:
* **Mitigation (Prevention):** What are you doing right now so this bad thing *doesn't* happen?
* **Contingency (Reaction):** The bad thing happened anyway. How are you going to fix it or recover?

*(Note: This is also dependent on your university requirements—some just need the mitigations!)*

## One major common issue is: What if I identify more risks later?

This can be managed by keeping the original table and just adding more rows, but adding an additional column for "Extended vs. Initial". 

Or, you can color the rows differently, so the original ones are (for example) plain or yellow, and the additional ones are blue. This way, the reader can clearly see how your project management developed over time. 

***

**Final tip:** Don't stress too much about the exact numbers in the Likelihood and Impact columns. Just make sure the really disastrous things (like losing all your data) have high Impact scores! Also, do not overthink this table, just keep adding risks as you work on the project rather than trying to figure it all out from day zero.
